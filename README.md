The American Sovereign Wealth Fund for Debt Resolution
A Proposal for Long-Term Fiscal Solvency

The Problem-

The United States carries over $36 trillion in national debt with no credible plan to address it. Current interest payments alone exceed $1 trillion annually. Washington offers no serious structural solution — only continued borrowing. Default or resolution are the only two real outcomes. This proposal chooses resolution.

The window to act is closing faster than most understand. At current trajectory the national debt will exceed $50 trillion within the decade. Interest payments are already the fastest growing line item in the federal budget, surpassing defense spending. Every year this fund is not started is a year of compounding lost forever — and unlike the debt, lost compounding cannot be recovered. The time for study, debate, and delay has passed. This needs to start now. The United States does not exist in isolation — its fiscal stability underwrites the global financial system, allied security, and the basic functioning of world markets. An American default is not an American problem. It is a worldwide catastrophe. There is no safe harbor. There is no country to run to. On a global scale the choice is exactly this simple — we thrive together or we fail together. This proposal is how we choose to thrive.

The Mechanism-

A federally seeded sovereign wealth fund of $1 trillion is established and invested equally across three passive index funds — the Dow Jones Industrial Average, the S&P 500, and the NASDAQ. A portion is allocated to an international index purely as a risk hedge, ensuring the fund remains protected during any prolonged domestic market downturn.
The fund is supplemented annually through three contribution mechanisms.

The first is a progressive tiered corporate levy structured to include every American business without crushing smaller companies. Small businesses earning under $1 million in net income contribute 0.5% of net profit. Medium businesses earning $1 million to $50 million contribute 1%. Large private companies earning $50 million to $500 million contribute 2%. Large public companies earning $500 million or more contribute 3%. Mega cap companies in the top 100 publicly traded corporations contribute 4% of net profit. This levy generates approximately $250 billion annually.

The second is a financial transaction tax applied to all US equity trades. At 0.3%, applied to approximately $113 trillion in annual US equity volume, the tax generates roughly $339 billion per year. At 0.5% it generates roughly $565 billion per year. Both rates grow naturally over time without adjustment as market volume expands. This rate is not without precedent. The United Kingdom has charged exactly 0.5% since 1808 under its Stamp Duty Reserve Tax — London remains one of the two most consequential financial markets on earth. France currently applies 0.3%. South Korea and Switzerland apply rates at or near 0.3% on equity transactions. The United States itself maintained a financial transaction tax from 1914 to 1966 without market disruption.

The third is the existing 1% federal tax on corporate stock buybacks enacted in 2023, redirected to the fund. Buybacks reached a record $942 billion in 2024 and are projected to exceed $1.2 trillion in 2025, generating approximately $10 billion annually. This figure is included in the structure but not in the projections below.

No active management. No market timing. Pure compounding.

The Projections-

Based on real 10 year market data reflecting an 11% average annual return across the three indexes. 

At the 0.3% transaction tax rate with total annual contributions of approximately $589 billion, the fund projects as follows.

10 years: $14.1 trillion.

20 years: $49.8 trillion.

30 years: $143.7 trillion.

At the 0.5% transaction tax rate with total annual contributions of approximately $815 billion, the fund projects as follows.

10 years: $18.1 trillion.

20 years: $65.8 trillion.

30 years: $192.4 trillion.

Both projections grow naturally over time as equity trading volume and buyback activity expand. At year 30 under either scenario the fund does not merely approach the national debt — it buries it.

The Governance-
The fund is managed by an independent board whose seats are automatically assigned to existing nonpolitical roles — the Fed Chair, the CBO Director, the Comptroller General, and appointed independent economists. No Presidential nomination. No political capture. Members serve staggered 14 year terms and can only be removed for criminal conduct. Congress cannot appropriate, redirect, or liquidate the fund under any circumstance. The fund's sole mandate is passive index investment until the national debt is resolved.
Why This Is Different
Every other debt proposal requires cutting — which requires political will that has never materialized. This proposal builds. It uses the same passive index investing strategy that has generated wealth reliably for decades and applies it at national scale. Those who have benefited most from the system that created the debt — large corporations, high net worth individuals, and the financial industry — contribute proportionally to resolving it. The contribution rates are deliberately nominal, structured so that the pain is negligible and the alternative — default — is catastrophic for the very entities being asked to contribute. The fund is insulated from the political cycle by design. It simply needs time and discipline — two things Washington has never managed, which is precisely why this structure removes Washington from the equation entirely.
