The American Sovereign Wealth Fund for Debt Reduction
A Proposal for Long-Term Fiscal Solvency

The Problem-
The United States carries over $36 trillion in national debt with no credible plan to address it. Current interest payments alone exceed $1 trillion annually. Washington offers no serious structural solution — only continued borrowing. Default or resolution are the only two real outcomes. This proposal chooses resolution.

The Mechanism-
A federally seeded sovereign wealth fund of $1 trillion is established and invested equally across three passive index funds — the Dow Jones Industrial Average, the S&P 500, and the NASDAQ. A portion is allocated to an international index purely as a risk hedge, ensuring the fund remains protected during any prolonged domestic market downturn. The fund is supplemented annually by $250 billion generated through a progressive tiered corporate debt levy and an individual wealth levy on net worth exceeding $10 million.
Corporate contributions are structured to include every American business without crushing smaller companies. 

Small businesses earning under $1M in net income contribute 0.5% of net profit. 

Medium businesses earning $1M to $50M contribute 1%. 

Large private companies earning $50M to $500M contribute 2%. 

Large public companies earning $500M or more contribute 3%. 

Mega cap companies in the top 100 publicly traded corporations contribute 4% of net profit. 
No active management. No market timing. Pure compounding.

The Projections-
Based on real 10 year market data reflecting an 11% average annual return across the three indexes, the fund projects as follows. 

10 years: $7.9 trillion.

20 years: $25.1 trillion.

30 years: $68.4 trillion. 

At year 30 the fund approaches nearly double the current national debt — built from a one time seed, modest broad based contributions, and uninterrupted compounding.

The Governance-
The fund is managed by an independent board whose seats are automatically assigned to existing nonpolitical roles — the Fed Chair, the CBO Director, the Comptroller General, and appointed independent economists. No Presidential nomination. No political capture. Members serve staggered 14 year terms and can only be removed for criminal conduct. Congress cannot appropriate, redirect, or liquidate the fund under any circumstance. The fund's sole mandate is passive index investment until the national debt is resolved.

Why This Is Different-
Every other debt proposal requires cutting — which requires political will that has never materialized. This proposal builds. It uses the same passive index investing strategy that has generated wealth reliably for decades and applies it at national scale. Those who have benefited most from the system that created the debt — large corporations and high net worth individuals — contribute proportionally to resolving it. The fund is insulated from the political cycle by design. It simply needs time and discipline — two things Washington has never managed, which is precisely why this structure removes Washington from the equation entirely.


UPDATE:

I am writing to follow up on the American Sovereign Wealth Fund proposal I submitted recently, and to correct a significant oversight on my part.
The original proposal projected the fund reaching approximately $68.4 trillion at year 30, based on an 11% average annual return across the three index funds. That number was accurate as a projection. What I failed to account for was what $68.4 trillion actually needs to be worth in 30 years — not what it accumulates to in raw dollars, but whether it is sufficient relative to where the national debt and broader fiscal picture will be at that point. At conservative to moderate return assumptions, the fund as originally structured falls short of what is needed in real terms.
Since submitting the original proposal I have revisited the contribution framework entirely and believe the revised structure below resolves that problem decisively.
The original $1 trillion federal seed and the progressive tiered corporate levy generating approximately $250 billion annually remain unchanged. What I am adding is a financial transaction tax on US equity trades, modeled at two rates for consideration.

At 0.3%, applied to approximately $113 trillion in annual US equity volume, the tax generates roughly $339 billion per year, bringing total annual contributions to approximately $589 billion. At that level the fund projects as follows. 

10 years, $14.1 trillion. 

20 years, $49.8 trillion. 

30 years, $143.7 trillion.

At 0.5%, the tax generates roughly $565 billion per year, bringing total annual contributions to approximately $815 billion. At that level the fund projects as follows.

10 years, $18.1 trillion. 

20 years, $65.8 trillion. 

30 years, $192.4 trillion.

Both projections assume 11% average annual return consistent with real 10 year index data used in the original proposal.
The 0.5% rate is not without precedent. The United Kingdom has charged exactly this rate since 1808 under its Stamp Duty Reserve Tax. London remains one of the two most consequential financial markets on earth. France currently applies 0.3%. South Korea and Switzerland apply rates at or near 0.3% on equity transactions. The United States itself maintained a financial transaction tax from 1914 to 1966 without market disruption. Additionally, Congress enacted a 1% tax on corporate stock buybacks in 2023 — buybacks reached a record $942 billion in 2024 and are projected to exceed $1.2 trillion in 2025 — generating approximately $10 billion annually that could be redirected to the fund. This figure is noted but not included in the projections above.
All three return scenarios grow naturally over time without rate increases, as equity trading volume and buyback activity are both projected to expand significantly in the coming decade.
I still believe this is the only structural solution on the table that does not depend on political will, spending cuts, or assumptions that have historically never materialized. The core architecture is sound. The contribution framework, as revised, produces numbers that do not merely approach the debt — they bury it.
I apologize for not catching the future value issue in the original submission and appreciate your time and consideration.
